# Workflow Discovery Methodology

## Purpose

This methodology is designed to understand how operational workflows actually behave inside real environments.

The goal is not to document ideal processes.

The goal is to discover:
- how work truly moves
- where ambiguity appears
- where operational ownership becomes unclear
- where execution slows down
- and where systems become difficult to maintain over time

---

# Phase 1 — Workflow Observation

The first step is understanding the workflow as it currently exists.

This includes:
- operational stages
- people involved
- decision points
- tools used
- information movement
- and execution dependencies

At this stage, the objective is not optimization.

The objective is visibility.

---

# Phase 2 — Dependency Mapping

Once the workflow becomes visible, dependencies are mapped.

This includes:
- hidden operational dependencies
- repeated manual decisions
- undocumented assumptions
- approval bottlenecks
- fragile AI-assisted stages
- and workflow coupling

The focus is identifying where the system depends too heavily on:
- individuals
- memory
- undocumented logic
- or unclear escalation paths

---

# Phase 3 — Boundary Identification

Operational systems become unstable when boundaries are unclear.

This phase identifies:
- where AI outputs should stop
- where human validation is required
- where escalation becomes necessary
- and where execution authority should exist

The goal is preventing uncontrolled workflow behavior.

---

# Phase 4 — Workflow Decomposition

Complex workflows are decomposed into:
- smaller operational stages
- isolated responsibilities
- traceable transitions
- and maintainable execution units

The purpose is reducing operational ambiguity and improving system reasoning.

---

# Phase 5 — Structural Redesign

Once the workflow is understood, a redesigned structure can be proposed.

This may include:
- stage restructuring
- approval redesign
- execution separation
- AI integration boundaries
- validation checkpoints
- dependency reduction
- and operational simplification

---

# Core Principle

A workflow becomes scalable only when it remains understandable.

Operational complexity is not caused only by system size.

It is often caused by:
- unclear ownership
- hidden dependencies
- fragmented execution
- and uncontrolled operational drift
